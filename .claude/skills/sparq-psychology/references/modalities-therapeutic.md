# Therapeutic Modalities Deep Reference (1-6)

These are the 6 core therapy-derived modalities that form the clinical backbone of Sparq's content. All are used educationally — never clinically.

---

## 1. Gottman Method

**Research basis**: John Gottman, 40+ years of longitudinal research at the University of Washington. Studied 3,000+ couples. Claims 94% divorce prediction accuracy from observing 15 minutes of conflict.

### Sound Relationship House (7 Levels)

1. **Love Maps** — How well you know your partner's inner world (dreams, fears, stresses)
2. **Fondness & Admiration** — Active culture of respect and appreciation
3. **Turning Toward** — Responding to bids for connection (vs. turning away/against)
4. **Positive Perspective** — Giving partner benefit of the doubt
5. **Manage Conflict** — Not resolving all conflict, but managing perpetual problems
6. **Make Life Dreams Come True** — Supporting each other's aspirations
7. **Shared Meaning** — Creating rituals, roles, goals, symbols together

### The Four Horsemen + Antidotes

| Horseman | Description | Antidote |
|---|---|---|
| **Criticism** | Attacking partner's character ("You always...") | Gentle startup: "I feel X about Y, I need Z" |
| **Contempt** | Disgust, mockery, superiority (strongest divorce predictor) | Build culture of appreciation; describe own feelings |
| **Defensiveness** | Counter-attacking or playing victim | Accept responsibility for even a small part |
| **Stonewalling** | Withdrawing, shutting down, going silent | Self-soothe, then return: "I need a break but I'm coming back" |

### Key Concepts for Sparq Content

- **5:1 Ratio**: Stable couples have 5 positive interactions for every 1 negative. Content should help users notice and create positive interactions.
- **Bids for Connection**: A "bid" is any attempt to connect (a look, a question, a touch). Partners can turn toward, turn away, or turn against. Sparq trains bid recognition.
- **Repair Attempts**: Any effort to de-escalate during conflict. Gottman's #1 predictor of relationship success is not conflict frequency — it's **repair speed and quality**.
- **Perpetual Problems**: 69% of couple conflicts are perpetual (never fully resolved). The goal is dialogue, not solution.
- **Dreams Within Conflict**: Behind every rigid position is a dream or deep personal meaning.

### How Sparq Uses Gottman

- **Conflict First Aid**: Four Horsemen awareness + repair starters
- **Weekly Mirror**: Tracks repair attempts and positive-to-negative ratio
- **Daily Actions**: Bid recognition exercises, appreciation practices
- **Skill Tree → Communication**: Gentle startup, turning toward bids
- **Skill Tree → Conflict Repair**: Repair attempts, de-escalation, return after stonewalling

## 2. EFT (Emotionally Focused Therapy)

**Research basis**: Sue Johnson, Ottawa Couple and Family Institute. Meta-analyses show 70-73% recovery rate for distressed couples. Based on attachment theory applied to adult romantic bonds.

### Core Framework

EFT views relationship distress as an **attachment crisis**. Partners aren't enemies — they're trapped in a **negative cycle** together. The cycle is the problem, not either person.

### Common Negative Cycles

| Cycle | Dynamic | What Each Partner Feels |
|---|---|---|
| **Pursue-Withdraw** | A presses for connection → B pulls away → A presses harder → B shuts down further | A: "They don't care." B: "I can't do anything right." |
| **Criticize-Defend** | A criticizes → B defends → A escalates → B stonewalls | A: "They won't listen." B: "I'm under attack." |
| **Attack-Counterattack** | Both partners escalate, matching intensity | Both: "I have to fight to be heard." |
| **Freeze-Freeze** | Both withdraw, avoiding all conflict | Both: "It's not worth the risk." |

### The EFT Process (Identify → Explore → Restructure)

1. **Identify** the negative cycle — name it without blame ("We're in the cycle again")
2. **Explore** the underlying attachment needs driving each position ("I pursue because I'm afraid you'll leave")
3. **Restructure** interactions by creating new, safer ways to express needs ("Can you tell me when you need space, so I know you're coming back?")

### Key Concepts for Sparq Content

- **Emotional Accessibility**: Being open to your own emotions and your partner's
- **Emotional Responsiveness**: Actively showing your partner their emotions matter to you
- **Attachment Needs**: Security, belonging, being valued, being seen — the needs driving "irrational" relationship behavior
- **The Cycle as the Enemy**: The most powerful EFT intervention — externalizing the pattern

### How Sparq Uses EFT

- **Partner Synthesis**: "You both" framing, blended reflection, cycle naming
- **Couple's Cycle Map** (planned): Names the couple's repeating pattern as a shared system
- **Evening Chat**: Peter explores underlying needs behind surface complaints
- **Morning Stories**: Demonstrate cycle awareness through Alex and Sam narratives
- **Identity Arc**: "You used to chase. I've watched you learn to wait with trust."

## 3. ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy)

**Research basis**: Steven Hayes, University of Nevada. Over 600 randomized controlled trials. Core construct: psychological flexibility — the ability to be present, open up, and do what matters.

### Six Core Processes

1. **Present Moment Awareness** — Being here now, not lost in past/future
2. **Acceptance** — Making room for difficult feelings without fighting them
3. **Cognitive Defusion** — Seeing thoughts as thoughts, not truths ("I'm having the thought that...")
4. **Self-as-Context** — You are not your thoughts/feelings; you are the awareness observing them
5. **Values Identification** — What matters most to you at your deepest level
6. **Committed Action** — Taking values-aligned action even when it's uncomfortable

### Key Concepts for Sparq Content

- **Values vs. Goals**: Values are directions (being a caring partner); goals are destinations (plan a date night). Values never complete — they guide ongoing action.
- **Willingness**: Choosing to feel uncomfortable feelings in service of what matters, rather than avoiding discomfort
- **The "Why" Anchor**: Connecting daily actions to personal values makes behavior change 3x more durable than rule-following
- **Defusion Techniques**: "I notice I'm having the thought that my partner doesn't care" (creates distance from thought)

### How Sparq Uses ACT

- **Values Clarification Module** (planned): "What kind of partner do you want to be in 5 years?"
- **Daily Loop**: Every micro-action is framed as a "vote for who you're becoming" (values-based)
- **Peter's Language**: References user's stated values in hard moments
- **Psychological Flexibility**: Morning stories demonstrate acceptance of difficult feelings + values-aligned action

## 4. CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy)

**Research basis**: Aaron Beck, University of Pennsylvania (1960s-present). Most extensively studied psychotherapy. Core insight: thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are interconnected — change one, and you shift the others.

### The Thought-Feeling-Behavior Triangle

```
        Thought
       /       \
      /         \
   Feeling ---- Behavior
```

Example in relationships:
- **Thought**: "They didn't text back — they must not care about me"
- **Feeling**: Hurt, anxiety, anger
- **Behavior**: Send an accusatory follow-up text / withdraw and stonewall

### Common Cognitive Distortions in Relationships

| Distortion | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Mind Reading** | Assuming you know what partner thinks/feels | "I know they're annoyed with me" |
| **Catastrophizing** | Assuming the worst outcome | "If we disagree about this, the relationship is over" |
| **All-or-Nothing** | Seeing in black and white | "You never listen to me" / "You always do this" |
| **Emotional Reasoning** | Treating feelings as facts | "I feel unloved, so I must be unloved" |
| **Personalization** | Taking everything as about you | "They're in a bad mood — it must be something I did" |
| **Should Statements** | Rigid rules about how partner should behave | "A good partner should know what I need without asking" |
| **Negative Filter** | Only noticing the negative | Ignoring 10 nice things, fixating on 1 criticism |

### How Sparq Uses CBT

- **Peter's Mirroring Effect**: When users use "always/never/every time," Peter gently challenges the absolute ("Is there any time recently they didn't?")
- **Reframing in Morning Stories**: Stories demonstrate seeing the same situation from a more generous perspective
- **Translator Feature**: Rephrases messages using Thought-Feeling-Behavior awareness
- **Daily Questions**: "What's another way to read what happened?" (cognitive restructuring)

## 5. IFS (Internal Family Systems)

**Research basis**: Richard Schwartz (1990s-present). Listed by NREPP as evidence-based. Core insight: the mind is naturally multiple — we all have "parts" that carry different roles and burdens.

### Parts Framework

| Part Type | Role | In Relationship Conflict |
|---|---|---|
| **Managers** | Protect by controlling, planning, criticizing | "I need to make sure this goes right" / criticism of partner |
| **Firefighters** | React urgently to suppress pain | Rage, numbing, distraction, substance use, stonewalling |
| **Exiles** | Carry the original pain/shame/fear | "I'm unlovable" / "I'll be abandoned" |
| **Self** | Core, calm, curious, compassionate leadership | Choosing to respond rather than react |

### Key Concepts for Sparq Content

- **Self-Leadership**: Responding from the Self (curious, calm, compassionate) rather than from a reactive part
- **Unblending**: Recognizing "that's a part of me speaking, not all of me"
- **Noticing Parts Without Judgment**: "Part of me wants to yell right now. That makes sense — it's trying to protect me."
- **Parts in Couple Conflict**: When both partners are "blended" with protective parts, the real people are absent

### How Sparq Uses IFS

- **Evening Reflection**: "What part of you showed up in that moment? What was it trying to protect?"
- **Conflict First Aid**: "Notice what's activated right now. That's a part — not all of you."
- **Peter's Language**: "It sounds like part of you really wanted to protect yourself there. That makes sense."
- **Daily Actions**: Self-leadership practices — pausing to notice which "part" is running the show

## 6. Narrative Therapy

**Research basis**: Michael White and David Epston (1980s-present). Postmodern therapeutic approach. Core insight: the stories we tell about ourselves shape our identity and behavior.

### Key Principles

- **Externalization**: "The problem is the problem, not the person." Separate the person from their pattern.
- **Dominant vs. Alternative Stories**: People carry a dominant story ("I'm bad at communication") that obscures alternative stories ("I had that one great conversation last Tuesday")
- **Unique Outcomes**: Moments that contradict the dominant story — evidence that change is already happening
- **Re-Authoring**: Helping users construct a new, preferred story about who they are

### How Sparq Uses Narrative Therapy

- **Morning Stories**: Alex and Sam narratives externalize relationship challenges — the couple faces the problem together, not each other
- **Identity Arc Tracking** (planned): Peter narrates the user's growth trajectory: "You used to... I've watched you..."
- **Graduation Report**: Re-authors the user's self-story based on 14 days of evidence
- **Weekly Mirror**: "I noticed you..." language — finding unique outcomes in the user's own behavior
- **Peter's Core Move**: Always frame traits as patterns-in-progress, never fixed labels. "You tend to step back" → "You used to step back, and I've watched you stay present three times this week."
