# Exercise Templates for the Daily Loop

Each template follows a consistent structure. Exercises are designed for the Daily Loop (2-5 minutes) and tagged by type: Individual, Partner, or Combined.

---

## Template Structure

```
Title:              Short, inviting name
Modality:           Which framework this draws from
Type:               Individual / Partner / Combined
Time Estimate:      2-5 minutes
Difficulty:         Beginner / Intermediate / Advanced
Instructions:       Clear, numbered steps
Reflection Prompt:  Self-reflection question after completing
Partner Sharing:    (Optional) How to share the experience with a partner
Why This Matters:   Accessible science context
```

## Individual Exercises

### 1. The Appreciation Scan (Positive Psychology)

**Type**: Individual | **Time**: 2 min | **Difficulty**: Beginner

**Instructions**:
1. Close your eyes for 10 seconds. Think about the last 24 hours with your partner.
2. Find one moment — even tiny — where they did something thoughtful, kind, or helpful.
3. Now get specific: what exactly did they do? How did it make you feel?
4. If you can, tell them today. If not, hold onto it — you'll carry it differently.

**Reflection prompt**: "What did I notice when I went looking for something positive?"

**Partner sharing**: "I noticed something you did yesterday that I really appreciated — [specific thing]."

**Why this matters**: Gratitude research (Emmons & McCullough, 2003) shows that actively scanning for positives rewires the brain's negativity bias. Couples who express specific gratitude report 25% higher relationship satisfaction.

---

### 2. The Thought Audit (CBT)

**Type**: Individual | **Time**: 3 min | **Difficulty**: Intermediate

**Instructions**:
1. Think about something your partner did recently that bothered you.
2. Write down (or think through) your first automatic thought about it.
3. Now ask: "Is there another way to read this situation?" Come up with at least one alternative interpretation.
4. Notice: which interpretation feels more true, and which one is more kind?

**Reflection prompt**: "What surprised me about the alternative interpretation?"

**Why this matters**: Aaron Beck's research shows that our first interpretation of ambiguous partner behavior is often distorted by mood, fatigue, or old patterns. Generating alternatives doesn't mean the first thought is wrong — it means you're giving your partner the same benefit of the doubt you'd want.

---

### 3. The Parts Check-In (IFS)

**Type**: Individual | **Time**: 3 min | **Difficulty**: Intermediate

**Instructions**:
1. Think about a recent moment when you reacted more strongly than the situation warranted.
2. Instead of judging yourself, get curious: what "part" of you showed up? Give it a name if you want (The Protector, The Fixer, The Escape Artist).
3. Ask that part: "What were you trying to protect me from?"
4. Thank it. It learned to do that for a reason.

**Reflection prompt**: "What was this part trying to protect? What does the more vulnerable feeling underneath look like?"

**Why this matters**: Richard Schwartz's research shows that our reactive behaviors aren't flaws — they're protective strategies. When we understand what they're protecting, we can choose to respond differently without fighting ourselves.

---

### 4. The Body Check-In (Somatic)

**Type**: Individual | **Time**: 2 min | **Difficulty**: Beginner

**Instructions**:
1. Pause wherever you are. Take one slow, deep breath.
2. Scan from head to toes. Where do you feel tension? Jaw, shoulders, chest, stomach, hands?
3. Name what you find without fixing it. "Tight shoulders. Clenched jaw. Flutter in my chest."
4. Take one more breath. Let your shoulders drop half an inch.

**Reflection prompt**: "What is my body holding right now that I wasn't aware of?"

**Why this matters**: Research shows emotions appear in the body 0.5-2 seconds before we consciously recognize them. Regular body check-ins help you catch stress or frustration before it drives reactive behavior with your partner.

---

### 5. The Values Anchor (ACT)

**Type**: Individual | **Time**: 3 min | **Difficulty**: Beginner

**Instructions**:
1. Complete this sentence: "The kind of partner I want to be is someone who..."
2. Now think about today. What's one small action you could take that would be a "vote" for that kind of partner?
3. It doesn't have to be big. A text, a pause, a question, a hug.
4. Do it before the day ends.

**Reflection prompt**: "What did it feel like to act from who I want to be, rather than how I felt in the moment?"

**Why this matters**: ACT research shows that values-driven behavior is 3x more durable than behavior driven by rules or willpower. When your daily actions align with the partner you want to become, change sticks.

---

### 6. The Story Rewrite (Narrative Therapy)

**Type**: Individual | **Time**: 4 min | **Difficulty**: Advanced

**Instructions**:
1. Think of a story you carry about yourself as a partner. Maybe "I'm bad at conflict" or "I always say the wrong thing."
2. Now search your memory for ONE moment that contradicts that story. Even a partial exception counts.
3. Describe that moment to yourself in detail. What did you do? How did it turn out?
4. Ask yourself: "What does this exception tell me about who I'm becoming?"

**Reflection prompt**: "If I wrote a new story based on my exceptions rather than my failures, what would the title be?"

**Why this matters**: Michael White's research shows that we live into the stories we tell about ourselves. When you find counter-evidence to a limiting story, you weaken its hold and open space for a preferred identity to emerge.

## Partner Exercises

### 7. The Bid Spotter (Gottman)

**Type**: Partner | **Time**: 5 min | **Difficulty**: Beginner

**Instructions**:
1. For the next hour, watch for moments your partner reaches out — a comment, a look, a question, a touch, a sigh, showing you something on their phone.
2. Each one is a "bid" for connection. Your job is simply to notice them.
3. When you spot one, turn toward it: respond, engage, even briefly.
4. At the end of the hour, count how many bids you noticed.

**Reflection prompt**: "How many bids did I notice? How many might I have missed on a normal day?"

**Partner sharing**: "I was paying extra attention today to the moments you reached out. I noticed [X]. I want to get better at catching those."

**Why this matters**: Gottman's research found that happy couples turn toward each other's bids 86% of the time. Unhappy couples: 33%. The difference isn't dramatic gestures — it's responding to the small, everyday attempts to connect.

---

### 8. The NVC Conversation (NVC)

**Type**: Partner | **Time**: 5 min | **Difficulty**: Intermediate

**Instructions**:
1. Pick a minor recurring friction (not your biggest issue — start small).
2. Using this structure, share with your partner:
   - "When [specific thing happens]..." (observation — just facts)
   - "I feel [emotion word]..." (not "I feel like you..." — that's a thought)
   - "Because I need [connection/respect/to feel important/etc.]..."
   - "Would you be willing to [specific, doable request]?"
3. Then switch — your partner shares theirs with the same structure.
4. No fixing, no defending. Just listen and reflect back.

**Reflection prompt**: "What was different about this conversation compared to how we usually discuss this?"

**Why this matters**: Marshall Rosenberg's research shows that when we separate observation from judgment and express needs instead of complaints, our partner hears information instead of criticism. The same truth, delivered differently, gets a completely different response.

---

### 9. Synchronized Breathing (Somatic)

**Type**: Partner | **Time**: 3 min | **Difficulty**: Beginner

**Instructions**:
1. Sit facing each other, close enough to hold hands (optional).
2. One person leads: breathe in for 4 counts, out for 6 counts.
3. The other person matches the rhythm.
4. After 1 minute, switch who leads.
5. Finish with 30 seconds of breathing together naturally.

**Reflection prompt**: "What changed between us in those 3 minutes?"

**Why this matters**: Research on co-regulation shows that partners' nervous systems synchronize. When you breathe together, your stress hormones drop and oxytocin rises. This is one of the fastest ways to shift from disconnection to safety.

---

### 10. The Vulnerability Exchange (EFT + Attachment)

**Type**: Partner | **Time**: 5 min | **Difficulty**: Advanced

**Instructions**:
1. Each partner completes this sentence privately: "Something I sometimes feel in our relationship but haven't said is..."
2. Partner A shares first. Partner B listens without responding for 30 seconds — just taking it in.
3. Partner B reflects back: "I hear you saying..." (no fixing, no defending).
4. Switch roles.
5. End with one sentence of appreciation: "Thank you for telling me that."

**Reflection prompt**: "What was it like to be truly heard? What was it like to truly hear?"

**Partner sharing**: Built into the exercise.

**Why this matters**: Sue Johnson's research shows that emotional accessibility (being open to feelings) and responsiveness (showing your partner their feelings matter) are the two strongest predictors of secure attachment. This exercise practices both.

## Combined Exercises (Individual prep → Partner share)

### 11. The Dream Beneath the Conflict (Gottman + EFT)

**Type**: Combined | **Time**: 5 min (3 individual + 2 partner) | **Difficulty**: Advanced

**Instructions — Individual prep (3 min)**:
1. Think about an ongoing disagreement you keep having.
2. Underneath your position, what's the dream or value driving it? What does it mean to you personally?
3. Write: "This matters to me because..."

**Instructions — Partner share (2 min)**:
4. Share what you wrote. Start with: "This issue keeps coming up, and I realized what's underneath it for me is..."
5. Partner listens without problem-solving. Just: "I didn't know it meant that to you."

**Reflection prompt**: "Did understanding the 'why' change how this disagreement feels?"

**Why this matters**: Gottman's research shows that 69% of couple conflicts are perpetual — they never get fully resolved. But couples who understand the dream behind each other's position navigate these conflicts with grace instead of gridlock.

---

### 12. The Identity Letter (Narrative + ACT)

**Type**: Combined | **Time**: 5 min (4 individual + 1 partner) | **Difficulty**: Advanced

**Instructions — Individual prep (4 min)**:
1. Write a short letter (3-5 sentences) from future you — the partner you're becoming — to present you.
2. Start with: "Dear [name], I'm writing from a year from now. Here's what I want you to know..."
3. Include: one thing you've learned, one thing you're proud of, one thing you want to keep practicing.

**Instructions — Partner share (1 min)**:
4. Read your letter out loud to your partner, or share the part that feels most important.

**Reflection prompt**: "Who am I becoming? What does this version of me do differently?"

**Why this matters**: Both ACT and Narrative Therapy research show that when people articulate a preferred future self, they begin living into that identity. Writing from your future self creates a bridge between who you are now and who you want to become.

## Exercise Selection Guidelines

### The Learn → Implement → Reflect Cycle

Every daily session follows this three-phase pedagogical structure:

1. **Learn** (Morning — 2 min): Story or psycho-educational content from the day's modality. The user discovers a concept through narrative, not lecture.
2. **Implement** (During the day): A specific micro-action to practice in real life. Always concrete and doable.
3. **Reflect** (Evening — 3 min): Peter facilitates processing. "How did it go? What did you notice?" This is where emotional integration happens.

Exercises should be designed to fit one of these three phases. "Learn" exercises are stories and insights. "Implement" exercises are the action templates below. "Reflect" exercises are the reflection prompts attached to each template.

### Modality Sequencing

| Days | Modality | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 1-3 | **Positive Psychology** | Non-threatening, strengths-based, builds rapport |
| 4-7 | **Attachment Theory + Gottman** | The "aha" hook — users discover their patterns |
| 8-14 | **Rotating all modalities** | AI selects based on emerging user profile |
| 15+ | **AI-driven** | Full personalization based on traits, skill tree track, growth areas |

### Day Phase → Exercise Difficulty

| Day Phase | Appropriate Exercises |
|---|---|
| Days 1-3 | Beginner Individual only (build safety and habit) |
| Days 4-7 | Beginner Individual + Beginner Partner |
| Days 8-10 | Intermediate Individual + Beginner/Intermediate Partner |
| Days 11-14 | Intermediate/Advanced Individual + Partner + Combined |
| Skill Tree | Full range, matched to track and level |

### User State → Exercise Adaptation

| User State | Exercise Adaptation |
|---|---|
| Struggling | Individual only, Beginner, Somatic/Mindfulness focus |
| Neutral | Standard mix, progressive difficulty |
| Thriving | Challenge with Advanced, Partner/Combined exercises |
| No partner | Individual exercises only, adapt partner prompts to self-reflection |
| Post-conflict | Somatic first, then gentle Gottman repair exercises |
