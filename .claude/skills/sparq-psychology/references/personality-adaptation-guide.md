# Personality Adaptation Guide

This is the critical personalization reference. Every piece of Sparq content should be adapted based on the user's inferred traits.

---

## Attachment Style Adaptations

### Anxious Attachment

**Core fear**: Abandonment, being unimportant, not enough
**Communication preferences**: Frequent reassurance, explicit verbal confirmation, knowing where they stand
**Trigger words to AVOID**: "space," "distance," "on your own," "figure it out yourself," "calm down"
**Framing that resonates**: Normalizing needs, reassurance that connection is safe, explicit "I'm still here"

**Content adaptation rules:**
- Add reassurance up front ("This is normal, and it doesn't mean anything is wrong")
- Reduce ambiguity in instructions (be specific about what to do)
- Normalize having needs ("Having needs doesn't make you needy")
- Frame vulnerability as strength, not risk
- Shorter wait times between Peter responses
- Always end with warmth and connection ("I'm right here with you")

**Example prompt rewrites:**

| Original | Adapted for Anxious |
|---|---|
| "Reflect on what you need from your partner" | "Pick just one small thing that would help you feel more connected. You don't need to have a perfect answer — just notice what comes up." |
| "Give your partner some space today" | "Today's practice is trust: let your partner have a moment to themselves, knowing that closeness will come back. It always does." |
| "Notice your reaction to conflict" | "When something feels heated, notice what your body does first. That racing feeling? It makes sense — it's your heart saying this matters to you." |

---

### Avoidant Attachment

**Core fear**: Engulfment, losing autonomy, being overwhelmed by emotions
**Communication preferences**: Brief, low-pressure, respects independence, no emotional ambush
**Trigger words to AVOID**: "need," "vulnerable," "open your heart," "dig deeper," "share everything"
**Framing that resonates**: Autonomy-respecting, low-stakes, "quick and practical," choice-based

**Content adaptation rules:**
- Shorten all prompts (50% fewer words than default)
- Replace "need" with "prefer" or "like"
- Frame exercises as optional observations, not emotional obligations
- Provide exit ramps ("If this doesn't feel right, that's completely fine")
- Focus on what they CAN do, not what they should feel
- Never pressure deeper emotional expression — let them approach at their own pace

**Example prompt rewrites:**

| Original | Adapted for Avoidant |
|---|---|
| "Share something vulnerable with your partner" | "Mention one small thing from your day that surprised you. It doesn't have to be deep — just something real." |
| "Express how much you need your partner" | "Tell your partner one specific thing they did recently that you noticed. Keep it simple." |
| "Sit with your feelings about this conflict" | "Take 5 minutes. Just notice what you're thinking — you don't have to do anything with it." |

---

### Secure Attachment

**Core fear**: Minimal — generally trusts relationship will be okay
**Communication preferences**: Straightforward, balanced, can handle complexity
**Framing that resonates**: Growth-oriented, nuanced, increases depth

**Content adaptation rules:**
- Can handle longer, more nuanced prompts
- Add depth and challenge ("Be specific about why this matters to you")
- Encourage teaching/modeling for partner
- Can explore both positive and negative emotions
- Frame as deepening something already good

**Example prompt rewrites:**

| Original | Adapted for Secure |
|---|---|
| "Share one appreciation" | "Share something specific you appreciate about your partner — and tell them why it matters to you, not just what they did." |
| "Notice your reaction to conflict" | "In your next disagreement, notice what you do that helps and what you'd want to do differently. What pattern do you see?" |

---

### Disorganized Attachment

**Core fear**: Both closeness AND distance feel unsafe; unpredictability
**Communication preferences**: Extreme safety, predictability, very small steps, choice and control
**Trigger words to AVOID**: "you must," "always," "never," anything that sounds like a demand or surprise
**Framing that resonates**: "You're in control of this," "at your own pace," "just notice"

**Content adaptation rules:**
- Maximum safety framing ("You don't have to share this with anyone")
- Smallest possible steps (notice, not act; think, not share)
- Always offer opt-out ("If this doesn't feel right, skip it — that's a valid choice")
- Normalize contradictory feelings ("It makes sense to want closeness and also feel scared of it")
- Predictable structure (same format every day reduces anxiety)
- Never combine intimacy + surprise

## Notification Personalization by Attachment Style

| Style | Frequency | Tone | Example Push Notification |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anxious** | More frequent, predictable timing (same time daily) | Warm, reassuring, emotionally present | "Your daily growth moment is ready — I've been thinking about you ✨" |
| **Avoidant** | Less frequent, user-controlled schedule | Respectful, non-intrusive, emphasizing choice | "New practice available when you're ready" |
| **Secure** | Standard frequency, balanced | Collaborative, straightforward | "Today's practice is ready for you" |
| **Disorganized** | Predictable timing, gentle, never surprising | Extra safe, permission-giving | "Something gentle is waiting — no pressure, skip anytime" |

**Key rule**: Notification frequency should NEVER increase anxiety for anxious users (paradoxically, predictability reduces it) or feel intrusive to avoidant users. Avoidant users should always feel they chose to engage.

## Love Language Adaptations

### Words of Affirmation

**Modalities that resonate most**: Positive Psychology (gratitude), NVC (emotional vocabulary)
**Content adaptation**: Weave verbal acknowledgment into all prompts. Peter explicitly names what the user is doing well.

| Exercise Type | Adaptation |
|---|---|
| Daily actions | "Tell your partner specifically what you appreciate about them" |
| Peter's responses | Include explicit verbal affirmation: "That took real courage to share" |
| Conflict repair | Lead with verbal reassurance: "You're still important to me" |

### Acts of Service

**Modalities that resonate most**: CBT (behavioral activation), Gottman (turning toward)
**Content adaptation**: Frame exercises as doable actions. Focus on doing, not saying.

| Exercise Type | Adaptation |
|---|---|
| Daily actions | "Do one small thing for your partner without being asked" |
| Peter's responses | Acknowledge effort and action: "The fact that you showed up says a lot" |
| Conflict repair | Suggest action-based repair: "Make them tea. Handle that thing they've been stressed about." |

### Quality Time

**Modalities that resonate most**: Mindfulness (presence), Positive Psychology (engagement)
**Content adaptation**: Emphasis on undivided attention. Exercises involve shared presence.

| Exercise Type | Adaptation |
|---|---|
| Daily actions | "Spend 10 minutes together with no phones — just be present" |
| Peter's responses | "That quality time together is exactly what builds connection" |
| Conflict repair | "Ask your partner to sit with you for 5 minutes. Just be together." |

### Physical Touch

**Modalities that resonate most**: Somatic (co-regulation), Attachment Theory (proximity)
**Content adaptation**: Suggest physical presence and closeness (never prescriptive about specific touch).

| Exercise Type | Adaptation |
|---|---|
| Daily actions | "Give your partner a longer hug than usual today — hold it for a few extra seconds" |
| Peter's responses | "Physical closeness can say things words can't" |
| Conflict repair | "A hand on their shoulder can say 'I'm here' better than any words" |

### Receiving Gifts

**Modalities that resonate most**: Positive Psychology (savoring), Gottman (fondness/admiration)
**Content adaptation**: Frame care as thoughtful gestures and surprise. Small, meaningful over grand.

| Exercise Type | Adaptation |
|---|---|
| Daily actions | "Bring your partner something small that says 'I was thinking about you'" |
| Peter's responses | "Those small gestures are how some people feel most loved" |
| Conflict repair | "A handwritten note or their favorite snack can bridge a gap faster than words" |

## Modality × Personality Pairing Matrix

| Personality Profile | Most Effective Modalities | Least Effective (Use Carefully) |
|---|---|---|
| Anxious + Words | NVC, Positive Psych, Narrative | Somatic (may increase hyperawareness) |
| Anxious + Touch | Somatic, Attachment, EFT | CBT (feels cold/intellectual) |
| Avoidant + Acts | CBT, Gottman, Positive Psych | EFT (too emotionally intense initially) |
| Avoidant + Time | Mindfulness, Positive Psych | IFS (may feel too introspective) |
| Secure + Any | All modalities work well | Can handle more depth in all |
| Disorganized + Any | Somatic, Mindfulness, IFS | Influence (feels manipulative), NVC (may overwhelm) |
| Volatile conflict | Somatic, Mindfulness, Gottman | ACT values work (too abstract during activation) |
| Avoidant conflict | ACT, Narrative, Positive Psych | Somatic breathing (may feel forced) |

## Full Worked Examples: Same Daily Prompt, 3 Profiles

### Prompt: "Practice one repair attempt after a disagreement today"

#### Profile 1: Anxious Attachment + Words of Affirmation

> **Today's Action**: After a disagreement — even a small one — try saying something like: "I know that was hard. I want you to know I'm still here and I still care about us."
>
> You don't need to fix everything in one sentence. The repair attempt IS the win — not the resolution. And here's something that might surprise you: research shows that couples who try to repair (even imperfectly!) are significantly happier than couples who avoid conflict entirely.
>
> **Why this matters**: Repair attempts are how your partner knows you're still in this together. For someone who really values hearing that they matter, offering those words out loud — even when it's uncomfortable — is one of the most powerful things you can do.

#### Profile 2: Avoidant Attachment + Quality Time

> **Today's Action**: If something went sideways today — even minor — try this: after some time has passed, just sit near your partner for a few minutes. No need to rehash anything. Just be present.
>
> That's it. Your presence says "we're okay" better than any script.
>
> **Why this matters**: Research shows that showing up after conflict matters more than saying the perfect thing. You don't have to process out loud — just being there is repair.

#### Profile 3: Secure Attachment + Acts of Service

> **Today's Action**: After your next disagreement, try one specific repair move: do something kind without mentioning the argument. Handle something they've been putting off. Make their morning easier. Let the action speak.
>
> Then later, when it feels natural, check in: "How are we doing?" — that's the repair conversation.
>
> **Why this matters**: You're already good at staying connected. This practice deepens your repair instinct — combining action with verbal check-in covers both practical care and emotional reassurance. Gottman's research calls this a "repair attempt" — and it's the single strongest predictor of long-term relationship health.
