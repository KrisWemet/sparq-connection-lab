# Question Bank Patterns

Example questions organized by modality, with therapeutic intent, difficulty scaffolding, and "why this matters" context. Use these as templates when generating new content.

---

## Structure for Every Question

```
Question Text:     The actual prompt shown to the user
Modality:          Which of the 12 frameworks this draws from
Therapeutic Intent: What psychological shift this is designed to create
Difficulty:        Beginner / Intermediate / Advanced
Stage:             Which relationship stages this fits (new, established, struggling, thriving, individual)
Why This Matters:  1-2 sentence accessible science explanation
Expected Outcome:  What the user should walk away with
```

---

## 1. Gottman Method

### Beginner
**Q**: "What's one small thing your partner did recently that you appreciated — something you might not have mentioned?"
- **Intent**: Build fondness and admiration; practice noticing positive
- **Stage**: All
- **Why this matters**: Research shows that couples who regularly express appreciation are significantly more satisfied. Noticing small things rewires your brain to look for what's going well.
- **Expected outcome**: User identifies one positive moment, practices gratitude expression

### Intermediate
**Q**: "Think about the last time your partner reached out to you — a comment, a look, a question. How did you respond? Did you turn toward it, or did it slip by?"
- **Intent**: Bid recognition — awareness of connection attempts
- **Stage**: Established, struggling
- **Why this matters**: Gottman found that couples who "turn toward" each other's bids 86% of the time stay together. Couples who turn toward only 33% don't. Learning to notice bids is the first step.
- **Expected outcome**: User becomes aware of bids for connection in daily life

### Advanced
**Q**: "In your most recent disagreement, what was the first moment either of you tried to soften things — a joke, a touch, an apology, a change of tone? What happened after that attempt?"
- **Intent**: Repair attempt recognition and analysis
- **Stage**: Established, struggling, thriving
- **Why this matters**: The ability to repair after conflict is the #1 predictor of long-term relationship health — more important than how often you fight or what you fight about.
- **Expected outcome**: User recognizes repair attempts in their own conflict patterns

## 2. EFT (Emotionally Focused Therapy)

### Beginner
**Q**: "When you and your partner disagree, what do you usually do first — move closer (try to talk it out right away) or step back (need time before you can engage)?"
- **Intent**: Beginning awareness of pursue-withdraw dynamic
- **Stage**: All
- **Why this matters**: Most couples fall into a predictable pattern during conflict. Just noticing your pattern — without judging it — is the first step toward changing it.
- **Expected outcome**: User identifies their default conflict position

### Intermediate
**Q**: "Behind your frustration in your last disagreement, what were you really asking for? Not the surface issue — what did you need to feel from your partner?"
- **Intent**: Access underlying attachment need beneath surface complaint
- **Stage**: Established, struggling
- **Why this matters**: The surface argument is rarely the real issue. Underneath most relationship conflicts is a deeper question: "Are you there for me? Do I matter to you?" When you can name that deeper need, everything shifts.
- **Expected outcome**: User connects surface complaint to deeper attachment need

### Advanced
**Q**: "If you could name the repeating pattern between you and your partner in conflict — without blaming either person — what would you call it? What does the pattern look like from the outside?"
- **Intent**: Cycle externalization — the core EFT intervention
- **Stage**: Established, struggling
- **Why this matters**: When couples can see their conflict pattern as something that happens TO them both (not something one person does TO the other), blame decreases and empathy increases dramatically.
- **Expected outcome**: User externalizes the cycle, seeing it as a shared system

## 3. ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy)

### Beginner
**Q**: "What kind of partner do you want to be — not perfect, just the direction you want to grow toward?"
- **Intent**: Values identification — the "why" anchor
- **Stage**: All
- **Why this matters**: People who connect their daily actions to personal values sustain change 3x longer than those following rules. Your "why" is the engine that keeps you going when motivation dips.
- **Expected outcome**: User articulates a values-based direction for their relationship behavior

### Intermediate
**Q**: "What uncomfortable feeling do you tend to avoid in your relationship? What might happen if you could sit with it — just for a moment — instead of running from it?"
- **Intent**: Willingness — making room for discomfort in service of values
- **Stage**: Established, struggling, individual
- **Why this matters**: Most of our relationship-damaging behaviors (withdrawal, people-pleasing, defensiveness) are attempts to avoid uncomfortable feelings. Learning to tolerate discomfort is the gateway to authentic connection.
- **Expected outcome**: User identifies an avoided emotion and considers approaching it

### Advanced
**Q**: "Notice a thought you keep having about your partner or relationship — something like 'they don't care' or 'this will never change.' Now try adding: 'I'm having the thought that...' before it. What shifts when you hold the thought at arm's length?"
- **Intent**: Cognitive defusion — creating distance from unhelpful thoughts
- **Stage**: Established, struggling, individual
- **Why this matters**: We often treat our thoughts as facts. The moment you notice "I'm HAVING the thought that they don't care" (vs. "they don't care"), you create space to choose your response instead of reacting automatically.
- **Expected outcome**: User practices defusion, experiences distance from a sticky thought

## 4. CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy)

### Beginner
**Q**: "Think about a recent moment with your partner that bothered you. What was the first thought that popped into your head? Now ask yourself: is there another way to read that same situation?"
- **Intent**: Basic cognitive restructuring — challenging automatic thoughts
- **Stage**: All
- **Why this matters**: Our first interpretation of a partner's behavior is often the least generous one. Research shows that checking our assumptions can reduce unnecessary conflict by up to 40%.
- **Expected outcome**: User identifies an automatic thought and generates an alternative interpretation

### Intermediate
**Q**: "Do you ever catch yourself thinking in absolutes — 'they always,' 'they never,' 'every time'? Pick one recent example and ask: is this truly always/never, or is my frustration making it feel that way?"
- **Intent**: Identifying all-or-nothing thinking (cognitive distortion)
- **Stage**: Established, struggling
- **Why this matters**: Absolute language ("you ALWAYS" / "you NEVER") triggers defensiveness and shuts down conversation. Finding the exception opens the door to nuance and dialogue.
- **Expected outcome**: User recognizes absolutist thinking and finds counter-evidence

## 5. IFS (Internal Family Systems)

### Beginner
**Q**: "Think about the last time you reacted strongly to something your partner said. What part of you showed up — the protector? The fixer? The one who wanted to flee? What was that part trying to do for you?"
- **Intent**: Parts awareness — recognizing reactive parts without judgment
- **Stage**: All
- **Why this matters**: When we react strongly, it's usually a protective part of us that has taken over. Recognizing "that's a part of me, not all of me" creates space to choose a different response.
- **Expected outcome**: User identifies a reactive part and understands its protective function

### Intermediate
**Q**: "When you feel the urge to [criticize / withdraw / fix / defend] in an argument, pause and ask: what is this reaction protecting me from? What's the younger, more vulnerable feeling underneath it?"
- **Intent**: Accessing the exile beneath the protector
- **Stage**: Established, struggling, individual
- **Why this matters**: Our most frustrating relationship behaviors are usually protecting something tender underneath — fear of rejection, shame, feeling not enough. When we understand what's being protected, we can respond to the real need instead.
- **Expected outcome**: User connects protective behavior to an underlying vulnerability

## 6. Narrative Therapy

### Beginner
**Q**: "If your relationship had a story title right now, what would it be? Now imagine the title you'd WANT it to have. What's one thing that already fits the story you want?"
- **Intent**: Re-authoring — identifying the preferred story
- **Stage**: All
- **Why this matters**: The stories we tell about our relationships shape how we experience them. Finding even one piece of evidence for the story you WANT helps you start living into it.
- **Expected outcome**: User identifies a gap between current and preferred narrative, finds evidence for the preferred one

### Advanced
**Q**: "You might carry a story about yourself as a partner — maybe 'I'm bad at communication' or 'I always mess things up.' Can you think of one moment recently that contradicts that story? What does that exception tell you?"
- **Intent**: Finding unique outcomes that contradict the dominant story
- **Stage**: Established, struggling, individual
- **Why this matters**: We all carry stories about ourselves that filter what we notice. When you find a moment that doesn't fit the old story, you're discovering evidence that you're already more than you think.
- **Expected outcome**: User identifies a counter-narrative moment, weakens a limiting self-story

## 7. Positive Psychology

### Beginner
**Q**: "What's one thing that went well in your relationship this week — even something small? What did you or your partner do to make it happen?"
- **Intent**: Gratitude + attribution of agency
- **Stage**: All
- **Why this matters**: Couples who regularly reflect on what's going well show 25% higher satisfaction. And noticing what you DID (not just what happened) reminds you that you're an active builder of your relationship.
- **Expected outcome**: User identifies a positive moment and their role in creating it

---

## 8. Attachment Theory

### Beginner
**Q**: "When you're stressed or upset, do you tend to reach out to your partner for comfort, or handle it alone first? There's no wrong answer — just notice your pattern."
- **Intent**: Self-awareness of regulation preference
- **Stage**: All
- **Why this matters**: Understanding how you seek comfort when stressed helps you communicate your needs. It also helps you understand why your partner might respond differently than you expect.
- **Expected outcome**: User identifies their regulation preference without self-judgment

---

## 9. Mindfulness

### Beginner
**Q**: "The next time your partner is speaking, try listening without planning your response. Just hear their words. What do you notice when you stop composing your reply?"
- **Intent**: Mindful listening practice
- **Stage**: All
- **Why this matters**: Most of us listen while planning what to say next. When you truly listen without an agenda, your partner can feel it — and the quality of your conversation changes immediately.
- **Expected outcome**: User experiences the difference between reactive and present listening

---

## 10. NVC (Nonviolent Communication)

### Beginner
**Q**: "Think about something your partner does that frustrates you. Instead of judging it ('they're so inconsiderate'), try describing just the facts: what literally happens? Then name one feeling you have about it."
- **Intent**: Separating observation from judgment — NVC step 1-2
- **Stage**: All
- **Why this matters**: When we say "you're inconsiderate," our partner hears an attack. When we say "when the dishes sit overnight, I feel stressed," our partner hears information. Same truth, completely different impact.
- **Expected outcome**: User practices separating observation from interpretation

### Intermediate
**Q**: "Behind your frustration about [recent issue], what do you actually need? Not what you want your partner to do differently — what is the underlying need? (Connection? Respect? To feel important? Autonomy?)"
- **Intent**: Need identification — NVC step 3
- **Stage**: Established, struggling
- **Why this matters**: Most arguments are about strategies (what to DO), not needs (what you NEED). When you know your need, you can find many strategies — not just the one your partner isn't providing.
- **Expected outcome**: User identifies the need underneath the complaint

## 11. Somatic Approaches

### Beginner
**Q**: "Right now, do a quick body scan. Where do you feel tension? Shoulders? Jaw? Chest? Just notice it without trying to fix it. That's your body's way of holding what you're going through."
- **Intent**: Body awareness — connecting physical sensation to emotional state
- **Stage**: All
- **Why this matters**: Emotions live in the body first — often before we can name them. Checking in with physical sensations helps you catch stress, frustration, or sadness before it drives reactive behavior.
- **Expected outcome**: User develops body awareness habit

---

## 12. Influence Psychology

### Beginner
**Q**: "What's one small relationship commitment you're willing to make this week — something specific and doable? Write it down or say it out loud."
- **Intent**: Commitment/consistency — public microcommitment
- **Stage**: All
- **Why this matters**: When we state a commitment out loud or in writing, we're significantly more likely to follow through. Not because of willpower — because our brain naturally wants to act consistently with who we've said we are.
- **Expected outcome**: User makes a concrete, small commitment that leverages consistency bias
